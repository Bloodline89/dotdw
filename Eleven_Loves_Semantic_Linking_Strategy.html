How Semantic Internal Linking Works for SEO

Semantic internal linking connects pages based on their topical relationships, helping search engines understand your site's content structure and distributing ranking authority throughout your pages.

Link Equity Distribution

When one page links to another, it passes a portion of its "link equity" (also called PageRank or link juice) to the target page. Pages with more inbound links from authoritative pages accumulate more equity, signalling to Google that they're important.

  • Hub pages (e.g., /collections/dresses) with many inlinks become authority sources
  • Spoke pages (e.g., /collections/maxi-dresses) receive equity when linked from hubs
  • Orphan pages with few or no internal links struggle to rank regardless of content quality

Topical Relevance Signals

Google uses internal links to understand how pages relate to each other topically. When you link related pages together with descriptive anchor text, you create topical clusters that signal expertise in a subject area.

Strong Topical Signal
  • /collections/dresses → /collections/maxi-dresses
    Parent links to child variant - clear hierarchy
  • /collections/tops → /collections/blouses
    Category links to subcategory - topic reinforcement
  • /collections/workwear → /collections/womens-blazers
    Context links to product type - user intent match
Weak/No Topical Signal
  • Homepage → /collections/barrel-leg-trousers
    Direct link from homepage skips hierarchy
  • /collections/party → /collections/loungewear
    No semantic relationship between contexts
  • Footer links to all collections
    Site-wide links dilute relevance signals

Parent-Child Relationships

The most powerful semantic links establish clear parent-child relationships between categories and subcategories. This creates a logical hierarchy that both users and search engines can follow.

Relationship Type Example SEO Benefit
Parent → Child /collections/dresses → /collections/maxi-dresses Passes authority down, establishes hierarchy
Child → Parent /collections/maxi-dresses → /collections/dresses Breadcrumb path, confirms hierarchy to crawlers
Sibling ↔ Sibling /collections/maxi-dresses ↔ /collections/midi-dresses Creates topical cluster, cross-pollination of rankings
Complementary /collections/dresses → /collections/heels Style context, captures "complete outfit" intent

Crawl Depth & Discovery

Search engines discover pages by following links. Pages buried many clicks from the homepage may not be crawled frequently or may be perceived as less important. Effective internal linking keeps important pages within 1-3 clicks of the homepage.

1-2
Ideal click depth for key collections
3-4
Acceptable for niche subcategories
5+
Likely to be undervalued or not indexed

Anchor Text Relevance

The clickable text in a link (anchor text) tells search engines what the target page is about. Using descriptive, keyword-relevant anchor text is a direct ranking signal for the linked page.

  • "Shop wide leg trousers" tells Google the target is about wide leg trousers
  • "Click here" or "View more" provides no topical signal
  • Varied anchor text across multiple links is more natural than identical anchors
  • Over-optimised anchors (e.g., always "cheap dresses UK") can appear manipulative

Anchor Text Guidelines

Semantic Anchor Text Best Practices

❌ Avoid
  • "Click here"
  • "View more"
  • "See all"
  • "Shop now"
  • "Collection"
✅ Use Instead
  • "Shop tulle skirts"
  • "Explore our cargo trouser styles"
  • "Browse the complete knitwear collection"
  • "Find your perfect party dress"
  • "Recycled tulle maxi skirts"
